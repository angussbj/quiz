## Contested or Approximate Dates

### WW1

**Gallipoli Campaign start date (`1915-02-19`)**
The CSV uses 19 February 1915, which marks the opening naval bombardment of the Dardanelles forts — the start of the broader Gallipoli/Dardanelles campaign. The land campaign at Anzac Cove and Cape Helles began 25 April 1915. Both dates are defensible; the February date reflects the campaign's naval phase. The event ID and alternate name ("Dardanelles Campaign") support this reading.

**Zimmermann Telegram date (`1917-01-16`)**
The telegram was sent by German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann to the German ambassador in Mexico on 16 January 1917. It was intercepted by British intelligence on 17 January, decoded over subsequent weeks, and handed to the US government on 24 February 1917. The date 16 January (the date sent) is used in the CSV, which is the conventional historical date for this event.

**Russian February Revolution dates (`1917-03-08` to `1917-03-12`)**
These are Gregorian (New Style) calendar dates. Russia was still using the Julian (Old Style) calendar in 1917, so contemporary Russian records show 23–27 February. Both are correct depending on calendar convention; the CSV uses Gregorian dates consistently.

**Russian October Revolution date (`1917-11-07`)**
Same calendar convention as above. The Julian date was 25 October 1917; the Gregorian equivalent is 7 November 1917. The CSV uses the Gregorian date.

### WW2

**Battle of the Bulge end date (`1945-01-16`)**
The end date of 16 January 1945 reflects the day US First and Third Armies linked up at Houffalize, restoring the pre-offensive front line in that sector. This is the date used by the US Army Center of Military History. Some historians extend the campaign to 25 January 1945, when the last German forces withdrew behind their original start lines. Both dates are in use; 16 January is the more commonly cited official end date.

**Me 262 First Jet Combat (`1944-07-26`)**
On 26 July 1944, a Messerschmitt Me 262 of the Aufklärungsgruppe (reconnaissance variant, unarmed) carried out what is considered the first jet combat sortie, intercepting a de Havilland Mosquito over Munich. The first armed fighter combat mission by the Me 262 is sometimes dated to August 1944 (or more specifically the first aerial victory to 26 July 1944 by Leutnant Alfred Schreiber, depending on the source). The date in the CSV (26 July 1944) is supported by multiple sources as the first confirmed jet combat flight.

**Soviet Invasion of Manchuria end date (`1945-09-02`)**
The end date of 2 September 1945 aligns with the formal Japanese surrender ceremony aboard USS Missouri. Organised Japanese resistance in Manchuria effectively ended earlier (around 20 August 1945), but the campaign is conventionally dated to the formal surrender.
